01Executive Summary

AMSA is the Commonwealth statutory authority responsible for maritime safety and for Australia's aviation and maritime search and rescue (SAR) service, delivered through the Joint Rescue Coordination Centre (JRCC) in Canberra. AMSA's confirmed SAR aircraft are four crewed Bombardier Challenger 604 jets — no in-house RPAS/drone fleet was identified. AMSA has, however, published a policy on the regulatory treatment of uncrewed/autonomous vessels (maritime, not aerial).

02Why this matters

AMSA coordinates national SAR response and, in 2025 alone, responded to 253 distress incidents, assisting 994 people and saving 187 lives. Its role as coordinator (not necessarily direct RPAS operator) makes it an important stakeholder for any drone-based SAR capability aiming to plug into the national SAR system — the actual aerial search-and-locate work is often performed by state police, SES or volunteer marine rescue organisations under AMSA's coordination.

Notes

AMSA's role is largely coordination across a federated SAR system (AMSA + state/territory police + Australian Defence Force as the three national SAR authorities, supported by SES, volunteer marine rescue and others) rather than sole direct delivery — relevant when assessing where an RPAS capability would actually plug in operationally.

04Operational Context

AMSA operates under the Australian Maritime Safety Authority Act 1990. Its SAR service covers the Australian Search and Rescue Region — around 52.8 million square kilometres of land and sea. In 2025, AMSA responded to 253 distress incidents across maritime, aviation and land environments, assisting 994 people and saving 187 lives. AMSA's National Search and Rescue Council brings together 10 SAR authorities under an Intergovernmental Agreement.
