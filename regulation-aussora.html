01Executive Summary

AusSORA is CASA's Australian adaptation of the JARUS Specific Operations Risk Assessment (SORA) framework — specifically SORA 2.5 with local adjustments — used to assess BVLOS and other complex drone operations. AusSORA is defined in Advisory Circular 101-06 and became the required assessment method for new applications from 11 May 2026, replacing the temporary TMI 2024-03 ground-risk rules that had been in place since 2024. Running alongside AusSORA, CASA is also operating a Broad Area BVLOS trial (TMI 2025-03) offering four defined pathways that let qualifying operators self-assess larger pre-approved operating areas rather than seeking a new approval for each site. This is one of the fastest-moving areas of Australian RPAS regulation and should be re-verified frequently.

02Why this matters

BVLOS capability is the single biggest regulatory gate on Drone-as-First-Responder, wide-area disaster mapping, and most critical-infrastructure inspection programs — the highest-value public safety and CNI use cases on this platform. AusSORA and the Broad Area BVLOS trial directly determine how quickly and cheaply an agency or operator can move from line-of-sight trials to a scaled BVLOS program, so any sales or solution engineering conversation about DFR or dock deployments needs an accurate, current read of this page.

04Operational Context

CASA uses AusSORA to assess complex drone operations where existing risk-management processes don't already cover the risk — this includes most BVLOS applications. The assessment covers ground risk (using current ABS Census population-density data), the "operational volume" (the flight geography plus contingency volumes), containment, and air risk class (assessed via airspace encounter category). Mitigations are categorised as M1 (reducing population exposure within the operational volume) or M2 (reducing impact force on collision). Applicants provide KML geographic data as part of the application. Separately, the Broad Area BVLOS trial lets qualifying operators — assessed partly on key-personnel criteria — self-assess pre-approved larger operating areas across four pathways differentiated by aircraft size, speed and population density limits, reducing the need to seek a fresh CASA approval per site. As of CASA's most recent public reporting, the trial covered more than half a million hectares across 19 broad operating areas involving 8 organisations, with completed BVLOS applications up 57% year-on-year.
